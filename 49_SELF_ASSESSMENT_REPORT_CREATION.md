## BAGIAN 2: WRITING GUIDELINES

### 2.1 TONE & VOICE

**DO:**
- ✓ Be professional, confident, and concise
- ✓ Use evidence-based language ("Our assessment data shows..." / "Evidence of this includes...")
- ✓ Be honest about challenges while showing solutions ("We identified X as an area for improvement. Action taken: [description]. Result: [outcome].")
- ✓ Write in third person or passive voice where appropriate
- ✓ Use active voice for clarity and impact
- ✓ Quantify claims with specific numbers/percentages
- ✓ Celebrate successes while remaining humble
- ✓ Demonstrate self-reflection and continuous improvement mindset

**DON'T:**
- ✗ Make unsupported claims or exaggerations
- ✗ Use subjective language without evidence ("Our faculty are exceptional")
- ✗ Defensive tone or explain away problems
- ✗ Vague, flowery language ("Our program strives to provide excellence...")
- ✗ Repetitive or overly long sentences
- ✗ Use jargon without clear explanation
- ✗ Ignore or minimize weaknesses
- ✗ Make promises that can't be kept

**Example of WEAK Writing:**
"Our faculty are excellent teachers who care deeply about student success. Students love our program and learn a lot. We are committed to continuous improvement."

**Example of STRONG Writing:**
"Our faculty achieved an average teaching evaluation score of 4.2/5.0 across three years, with 92% of students rating faculty as 'excellent' or 'good' teachers. Student satisfaction survey shows 87% would recommend the program to a friend, and 96% believe faculty are invested in their learning. Two faculty completed certificate programs in active learning pedagogy in 2025, with course redesigns showing improved student engagement metrics."

---

### 2.2 EVIDENCE INTEGRATION

**Structure for evidence-based claims:**

```
CLAIM + EVIDENCE + INTERPRETATION + IMPLICATION

Example:
"Our program successfully achieves learning outcomes [CLAIM]. 
Assessment data shows 83% of graduating students meet proficiency on PLO 1, 
80% on PLO 2, 87% on PLO 3, exceeding our 80% target [EVIDENCE]. 
This demonstrates that students are developing the intended competencies [INTERPRETATION]. 
Our curriculum design effectively prepares graduates for professional practice [IMPLICATION]."
```

**When integrating data:**
- Always cite data source (e.g., "2025 Student Learning Outcomes Assessment Results" or "Three-Year Teaching Evaluation Analysis")
- Include specific numbers/percentages, not just trends
- Compare to target or benchmark when available
- Explain what the data means
- Connect data to program quality

**Avoid:**
- Presenting raw data without interpretation
- Cherry-picking only favorable data
- Making claims stronger than data supports

---

### 2.3 HANDLING WEAKNESSES & CHALLENGES

Framework for discussing areas needing improvement:

```
SITUATION + EVIDENCE + ROOT CAUSE + ACTION + EXPECTED OUTCOME

Example:
"In 2024, we identified lower-than-target performance in PLO 4 (Communication Skills) 
with only 75% of capstone students meeting expectations [SITUATION & EVIDENCE]. 
Root cause analysis revealed insufficient feedback on draft presentations [ROOT CAUSE]. 
In response, we implemented a mandatory mid-project presentation in fall 2025 with 
structured faculty feedback using a communication rubric [ACTION]. 
Preliminary results show improved student confidence; we anticipate reaching our 
80% target by the 2026 assessment cycle [EXPECTED OUTCOME]."
```

**This approach shows:**
- ✓ Honest self-assessment
- ✓ Systematic problem-solving
- ✓ Commitment to improvement
- ✓ Evidence of action and outcomes

---

### 2.4 SECTION-BY-SECTION WRITING TIPS

**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:**
- Lead with key program identity & mission
- Highlight 3 major strengths (with brief evidence)
- Name 2-3 strategic improvement areas
- End with confidence in program quality & direction

**OVERVIEW SECTION:**
- Establish program credibility (history, credentials)
- Connect to institutional mission
- Introduce distinctive features
- Set stage for detailed discussion in later sections

**CURRICULUM & LEARNING:**
- Show how curriculum is intentionally designed
- Use curriculum map/table to demonstrate alignment
- Highlight evidence of student learning (not just teaching activities)
- Connect assessment results to specific courses/interventions
- Show systematic approach to improvement

**FACULTY:**
- Lead with qualifications (degrees, credentials)
- Provide evidence of teaching quality (evaluation scores, innovation examples)
- Document research productivity (publications, grants)
- Show commitment to development (professional development, teaching improvement)
- Demonstrate engagement with students (mentorship, advising, research collaboration)

**STUDENTS:**
- Provide comprehensive enrollment data (trends, diversity)
- Demonstrate student success (graduation rates, achievement of learning outcomes)
- Show support systems in place (advising, tutoring, career services)
- Include student voice (satisfaction, feedback, outcomes)
- Highlight diversity of support for diverse students

**ASSESSMENT & EFFECTIVENESS:**
- Lead with assessment design (what, how, when, who)
- Present three-year assessment data in table format
- Interpret data: what does it mean? Are targets met?
- Provide specific examples of improvements made based on data
- Show feedback loop: data → decision → implementation → result

**RESOURCES:**
- Provide factual description of physical spaces, equipment, technology
- Connect resources to program delivery (show adequacy)
- Address any deficiencies with plans for improvement
- Show budget alignment with strategic priorities
- Highlight recent investments/updates
